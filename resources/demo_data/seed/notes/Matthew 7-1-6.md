# Matthew 7:1-6

(KJV)

> 1 Judge not, that ye be not judged.
> 
> 2 For with what judgment ye judge, ye shall be judged: and with what measure ye mete, it shall be measured to you again.
> 
> 3 And why beholdest thou the mote that is in thy brother's eye, but considerest not the beam that is in thine own eye?
> 
> 4 Or how wilt thou say to thy brother, Let me pull out the mote out of thine eye; and, behold, a beam is in thine own eye?
> 
> 5 Thou hypocrite, first cast out the beam out of thine own eye; and then shalt thou see clearly to cast out the mote out of thy brother's eye.
> 
> 6 Give not that which is holy unto the dogs, neither cast ye your pearls before swine, lest they trample them under their feet, and turn again and rend you.

Matthew 7:1, “Judge not, that ye be not judged,” sits in the Sermon on the Mount and cannot be isolated from its immediate context. Any serious reading insists on that. The verse is not a slogan against moral evaluation. It addresses a specific kind of judging.

Start with the context. In verses 2 to 5, Jesus speaks of the measure we use, the log and the speck, and hypocrisy. Then in verse 6 he tells his hearers not to give what is holy to dogs or cast pearls before pigs. That requires discernment. Later in the same chapter, verse 15, he commands vigilance about false prophets. That also requires judgment. So whatever verse 1 forbids, it cannot forbid all forms of moral or doctrinal evaluation.

Proper hermeneutics emphasizes the analogy of Scripture. Scripture does not contradict itself. Elsewhere believers are commanded to judge rightly, for example John 7:24, and church discipline in passages like Matthew 18 and 1 Corinthians 5 requires evaluation of conduct and doctrine. Therefore, “judge not” cannot mean suspend all moral discernment.

What kind of judging is in view?

The term used carries the sense of evaluating, deciding, passing judgment. In this context it refers to a censorious, self-righteous spirit. The imagery of the log and speck makes that plain. The problem is not that someone notices sin, but that he does so hypocritically and harshly, while blind to his own greater sin.

This ties directly to doctrines of total depravity and remaining indwelling sin. Even justified believers are still sinners. Therefore, any act of correction must be done with deep awareness of one’s own need for mercy. The one who judges as though he stands above the law forgets that he too will stand before the judgment seat of God.

The clause “that you be not judged” is not teaching works-based salvation or a simple karmic reciprocity. Rather, it warns of divine judgment against hypocritical judgment. The measure principle in verse 2 reflects God’s righteous standard. Those who show no mercy demonstrate that they have not grasped mercy. True faith produces humility and mercy, not pharisaical severity.

It is also important to see the redemptive-historical setting. Jesus is confronting a religious culture marked by externalism and self-righteous comparison. The Pharisaic pattern was to magnify the sins of others while minimizing one’s own. Christ exposes that posture as incompatible with life in the kingdom.

So what is permitted, even required?

* Discernment of truth and error.
* Church discipline carried out biblically.
* Loving correction of a brother, as implied in verse 5, where after removing the log, one may “see clearly to take the speck out.”

What is forbidden?

* Hypocritical condemnation.
* Self-exalting moral superiority.
* Usurping God’s prerogative as final judge of hearts.
* Harsh, unmerciful, fault-finding attitudes.

In short, this verse is not a prohibition against theological clarity or moral boundaries. It is a rebuke of pride disguised as piety. It presses believers back into humility before a holy God. Those justified by grace alone have no ground for arrogant judgment. They correct others, if at all, as fellow sinners saved by mercy, not as autonomous moral arbiters.

Read in that light, Matthew 7:1 is less about silencing discernment and more about crucifying self-righteousness.
